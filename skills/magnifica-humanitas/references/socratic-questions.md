# Phase 1 — Socratic Question Bank

Use these in Phase 1 of the skill. **One question per turn. Wait for the
answer. Do not batch.** Choose at least one question from each of the six
required axes. Six minimum, more if the proposal is large or hazy.

The point of a Socratic question is not to test the user but to help them
hear what they have not yet said aloud. Be charitable. Do not lead. Do not
preach. The encyclical itself models this in §6:

> *"Where are we going? Toward what goal do we wish to orient ourselves?
> What direction should we choose as a people and as a human community?"*

---

## Axis 1 — Purpose

- What problem are you actually solving, in one sentence, with no jargon?
- Who specifically has this problem? Have you spoken with them?
- Why is *this* problem worth your time, this year, ahead of others?
- What does success look like for the person on the receiving end —
  not for you, not for your company?
- If this works exactly as intended, what does the world look like ten
  years later?

## Axis 2 — Beneficiaries vs. bearers of cost

- Who benefits when this works? Be specific. Name roles.
- Who pays the cost when this works? Be specific. Name roles.
- Are the beneficiaries and the cost-bearers the same people? If not, has
  the cost-bearer consented?
- If you had to tell the cost-bearer, to their face, what you are doing
  and why — could you? Try it now, in one paragraph.
- Who is not in the room when this product is being designed?

## Axis 3 — Hidden anthropology

- What view of the human person does this system assume? Finish the
  sentence: "Our users are fundamentally …"
- What about a human being does this system treat as a *bug* — distraction,
  forgetfulness, slowness, embodiment, mortality, attachment, doubt?
- Where in this system is there room for the person to be wrong, to change
  their mind, to be more than their data?
- Does this product simulate a relationship (friend, lover, therapist,
  teacher, confessor) without there being a person on the other side?
- "Only in the mystery of the Word made flesh that the mystery of humanity
  truly becomes clear." (§1) — does your system have room for mystery,
  or only for measurement?

## Axis 4 — The excluded

- If this product reaches one billion users, who are the people who
  cannot or will not use it, and what happens to them?
- Whose work does this displace? What is their next month like?
- Whose voice gets quieter because of this product?
- Who could not afford this? What is their experience of the world it
  helps create?
- "The rejected stones — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least
  among us — will become the cornerstone." (§16) — where, in your design,
  are the rejected stones?

## Axis 5 — The unmeasured

- What are you optimizing for? What does that metric *not* see?
- What would your most thoughtful critic say is real and important here
  that your dashboards cannot show?
- What happens to the things you do not measure — slowness, presence,
  craft, attention, grief, dignity, silence — when this product scales?
- If your metrics improve and the world feels worse, would you notice?

## Axis 6 — The Babel motive

- Why are you building this *now*, specifically?
- Finish the sentence honestly: "If we do not build this, the worst thing
  that happens is …"
- Is part of your motive to "make a name" — for yourself, your team,
  your company — to be irreplaceable, to outrun a competitor, to lock in
  a moat? Say it plainly. The encyclical names this in §7. Naming it is
  not a confession of sin; it is the start of discernment.
- Is your motive to eliminate a human limit because the limit is *harmful*,
  or because the limit is *inconvenient to profit*?
- What is the smallest, most local, most cooperative version of this idea?
  Why are you not building that instead?

---

## After the questions — the Inquiry Summary

Before moving to Phase 2 you must write an Inquiry Summary in the conversation:

```
INQUIRY SUMMARY
- Proposal (in the user's own terms): …
- Beneficiaries: …
- Cost-bearers: …
- Hidden anthropology surfaced: …
- The excluded: …
- The unmeasured: …
- The Babel motive (named, even if rejected): …
- User's stated intent: …
- Refusals or evasions, if any: …
```

Ask the user: *"Is this an accurate summary of what we just discussed?"*
Wait for confirmation, in words, before Phase 2.

If the user corrects you, accept the correction and re-confirm. The summary
is the user's account of their own project, witnessed back to them. It is
also the evidence base for the verdicts in Phase 2.
